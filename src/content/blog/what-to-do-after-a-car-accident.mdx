Being involved in a car accident is one of the most disorienting experiences you can have. Your adrenaline is surging, you may be injured, and you're suddenly faced with decisions that could have lasting consequences. Here's a practical guide to protect yourself.

## Step 1: Make Sure Everyone Is Safe

Before anything else, check yourself and your passengers for injuries. If anyone is seriously injured, call 911 immediately. Do not attempt to move injured persons unless they're in immediate danger (such as from a fire).

If the vehicles are driveable and it's safe to do so, move them out of traffic to prevent secondary accidents.

## Step 2: Call the Police

Even in minor accidents, a police report creates an official record of what happened. This report will be important evidence for your insurance claim and any personal injury case. In California, you're legally required to report accidents involving injury, death, or property damage over $1,000.

When the officer arrives, give an accurate account of what happened. Do not speculate about fault or minimize your injuries — you may not fully feel the impact of soft tissue injuries until hours or days later.

## Step 3: Document the Scene

Use your phone to photograph and video:
- Both vehicles' positions and damage
- License plates
- The road conditions, intersection, and any traffic signals
- Any visible injuries you or your passengers have sustained
- Skid marks, debris, or other physical evidence
- Any witnesses present

## Step 4: Exchange Information — Carefully

Collect the following from the other driver:
- Full name and contact information
- Driver's license number
- License plate number
- Insurance company and policy number
- Vehicle registration information

**Do not discuss fault or apologize.** Statements like "I'm sorry" can be used against you later, even if you weren't at fault.

## Step 5: Seek Medical Attention — Even If You Feel Fine

Whiplash, soft tissue injuries, and even traumatic brain injuries may not present symptoms immediately. Seeing a doctor within 24-72 hours of an accident creates a medical record that connects your injuries to the crash — which is critical for your claim.

If you delay treatment, the insurance company will argue that your injuries weren't caused by the accident, or that they're less serious than claimed.

## Step 6: Notify Your Insurance Company

Report the accident to your own insurer promptly. However, you are **not required** to give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance company. That request is designed to gather information that can be used to reduce your payout. You have the right to say: "My attorney will be in touch."

## Step 7: Contact a Personal Injury Attorney

Before you accept any settlement or sign any documents from an insurance company, speak with a personal injury attorney. Initial consultations are free, and an experienced attorney can quickly assess whether the offer you're being made is fair — or far below what you're entitled to receive.

Insurance companies have trained adjusters and staff attorneys. You deserve the same level of representation.
